Basic Operations

The arithmetic symbols in R are identical to those in mathematics: addition (+), subtraction (-), multiplication (*), and division (/). In the R Console, type a formula as you would on paper, press Enter, and the result appears.

# The four basic operations
1 + 2
## [1] 3
3 - 4
## [1] -1
5 * 6
## [1] 30
7 / 8
## [1] 0.875
# Compound expressions
(2 + 4) / (5 - 3)
## [1] 3

A note on whitespace

You may have noticed that the formulas above place spaces between numbers and operators. This is purely a matter of readability — R itself is indifferent to whitespace. The expression (2+4) / ( 5- 3 ) runs perfectly despite its irregular spacing.

For human readers, however, well-placed spaces help identify code units. Hadley Wickham’s Tidyverse Style Guide recommends using spaces and blank lines to improve readability. This book follows that convention, and beginners are encouraged to develop the habit early. For inherited or third-party code, packages such as styler and air can reformat code in a single click.

Beyond the four basic operations, R supports a wider range of arithmetic: powers (^), modulo (%%), natural and base-10 logarithms (log(), log10()), and exponentials (exp()). Some operations are so common that they have dedicated functions named after their English abbreviations. Square root, for example, can be written as ^(1/2), but sqrt() (“square root”) is more common. The same logic applies to abs() (“absolute value”). This naming convention dates back to FORTRAN and C.

10^2
## [1] 100
5 %% 3      # remainder
## [1] 2
log(11)
## [1] 2.397895
log10(100)
## [1] 2
exp(12)
## [1] 162754.8
# Two ways to take a square root
9^(1/2)
## [1] 3
sqrt(9)
## [1] 3
# Absolute value
abs(-9)
## [1] 9

Other mathematical functions, though less frequently used, generally have R counterparts. Trigonometric functions are a case in point — note how the code reads almost like a passage from a math textbook:

sin(pi)
## [1] 1.224647e-16
cos(pi)
## [1] -1
tan(pi / 2)
## [1] 1.633124e+16
acos(1)
## [1] 0
asin(1)
## [1] 1.570796
atan(0.5)
## [1] 0.4636476

In addition, R pre-defines a number of useful numerical and character constants, available for direct reuse. The constant pi above is one example, representing \(\pi\). Other reserved words include the lowercase and uppercase Roman letters (letters, LETTERS) and month names and abbreviations (month.name, month.abb).

Try typing letters or month.name below and see what you get.

letters
month.name

Scientific Notation

In the example above, tan(pi / 2) returned 1.633124e+16. The expression of a number as the product of a real coefficient \(a\) (with \(1 \leq |a| < 10\)) and an integer power of ten is known as scientific notation, attributed to Archimedes. In computing systems, the letter e (“exponential”) connects the coefficient and the exponent: e+16 means “times \(10^{16}\)”. Thus \(200 = 2 \times 10^2\) may be written as 2e2, and \(0.002 = 2 \times 10^{-3}\) as 2e-3.

What does 2.8e+10 represent in plain numerals?

format(2.8e+10, scientific = FALSE, big.mark = ",")

Boolean Algebra

Beyond arithmetic, R relies heavily on Boolean algebra — a logical algebraic system proposed by the Irish mathematician George Boole. In R, the two truth values are represented by TRUE and FALSE (uppercase), abbreviated as T and F. In numeric contexts these values are coerced automatically: FALSE becomes 0 and TRUE becomes 1 (so TRUE + 1 evaluates to 2). Three Boolean connectives are central: & (and), | (or), and ! (not). The Boolean “empty set”, NULL, is also a fixed object (uppercase).

Boolean algebra in R is most often paired with comparison operators: equality (==), inequality (!=), greater than (>), less than (<), greater than or equal to (>=), and less than or equal to (<=). Each example below returns either TRUE or FALSE.

# Equalities
1 == 2
## [1] FALSE
1 == TRUE   # TRUE and FALSE behave as 1 and 0 with logical attributes
## [1] TRUE
0 != F
## [1] FALSE
# Inequalities
1 != 2
## [1] TRUE
1 > 2
## [1] FALSE
1 < 2
## [1] TRUE
1 >= 2
## [1] FALSE
1 <= 2
## [1] TRUE
# Boolean operations
3 > 2 & 2 > 1
## [1] TRUE
12 & 2 > 2
## [1] FALSE
-3 | 2 > 2
## [1] TRUE

Two points concerning the last three examples deserve attention. First, Boolean connectives are evaluated after their comparison operands: 3 > 2 & 2 > 1 first checks 3 > 2 and 2 > 1 (both TRUE), then computes TRUE & TRUE, which yields TRUE. Second, when a single value enters a Boolean expression — as in 12 & 2 > 2 and -3 | 2 > 2 — it is treated as a self-equality check; that is, 12 & 2 > 2 is equivalent to 12 == 12 & 2 > 2, and -3 | 2 > 2 to -3 == -3 | 2 > 2.

Why the double equal sign?

Beginners often find it odd that “equality” requires two equal signs (==). One reason is that in many programming languages, including R and Python, the single equal sign = is reserved for assignment (we shall return to this in Chapter 4). Adding a second equal sign turns it into a comparison, keeping it consistent with !=, >=, and <=.

You may forget at first and write = when you mean == — there is no need to chastise yourself; every learner passes through this stage. Practice will resolve it.

What is the result of the following expression?

3 > 2 > 1
# In R, the expression evaluates left to right:
#   (3 > 2) -> TRUE -> 1
#   1 > 1  -> FALSE
# So the final result is FALSE — not the meaning that mathematical notation would suggest.
# To obtain the mathematical reading, write:
(3 > 2) & (2 > 1)

Vectors

We now enter the realm of linear algebra, beginning with one of its foundational concepts: the vector. Mathematically, a vector is a directed sequence of numbers. The numbers from elementary algebra are, in linear-algebraic terms, scalars — quantities possessing magnitude but no direction.

In a math textbook, a vector takes the form \(\vec{a} = (11, 12, 13, 14, 15)\). In R, the same vector is constructed with a <- c(11, 12, 13, 14, 15), where c() stands for concatenate (or combine) — bundling elements into a single object. For consecutive integers the colon operator offers a shortcut: a <- 11:15. Mixed sequences are also possible, e.g. a <- c(1, 3, 11:15, 20:25). A third route is the sequence function seq() (English: sequence): a <- seq(from = 11, to = 15, by = 1). Because the step is one, this can be shortened to a <- seq(11, 15).

# Method 1: c() — the most general form
a  <- c(11, 12, 13, 14, 15)

# Method 2: the colon operator (consecutive integers only)
a1 <- 11:15

# Method 3: seq() — flexible step and length
a2 <- seq(from = 11, to = 15, by = 1)
a3 <- seq(11, 15)

# Verify that all four constructions yield identical results
a == a1
## [1] TRUE TRUE TRUE TRUE TRUE
a == a2
## [1] TRUE TRUE TRUE TRUE TRUE
a == a3
## [1] TRUE TRUE TRUE TRUE TRUE

If you read seq(from = 11, to = 15, by = 1) aloud — “a sequence from 11 to 15 increased by 1” — that is precisely what it does. Many R commands read this way. Master a few patterns of expressing intent in plain English and the act of programming begins to feel less foreign.

Indexing

In mathematical notation, an element of a vector is identified by a subscript: \(\vec{a}_2 = 12\), \(\vec{a}_5 = 15\), \(\vec{a}_{3,4} = 13, 14\). In R, this is accomplished with square brackets [], which extract a subset of the vector. The same mechanism becomes the foundation of data filtering in later chapters.

# Construct a demonstration vector
a <- c(11, 12, 13, 14, 15)

# Extract a single element by position
a[2]
## [1] 12
a[5]
## [1] 15
# Extract multiple elements via a position vector
a[c(3, 4)]
## [1] 13 14

The assignment arrow <-

The arrow <- is the most common assignment operator in R. A useful mnemonic is to view it as a “pictograph”: the arrow points left, indicating that the object on the right is stored into the name on the left. Once the vector (11, 12, 13, 14, 15) is saved as a, R thereafter regards a as that vector.

R is essentially the only popular language that uses <- for assignment; most others adopt the single equal sign. The reason traces back to S, R’s predecessor (introduced in Chapter 2). In the 1960s, a notation system called APL (A Programming Language) developed into a highly expressive language and inspired many descendants, including S. APL employed many special characters, so a dedicated keyboard was designed; <- was the left-arrow key in the upper-right corner of that keyboard. APL faded, the keyboard layout did too, but the <- habit was preserved by developers and lives on in R.

The arrow makes data flow visually intuitive — source on the right, destination on the left. It has also become a sort of badge of honour among R enthusiasts: only <- users count as true R users. R provides a shortcut for typing it: Alt + - on Windows / Linux, Option + - on macOS.

Vector Arithmetic

Vectors support addition, subtraction, and multiplication. The symbols (+, -, *) match those of elementary algebra. For addition and subtraction, corresponding elements are combined to yield a new vector:

\[\vec{a} \pm \vec{b} = (a_1 \pm b_1,\; a_2 \pm b_2,\; \ldots,\; a_n \pm b_n).\]

In R:

a <- c(11, 12, 13, 14, 15)

# Vector ± scalar
b <- a + 4
c <- a - 0.5

# Vector ± vector
a + b
## [1] 26 28 30 32 34
a - c
## [1] 0.5 0.5 0.5 0.5 0.5

For multiplication, the asterisk * performs element-wise multiplication, also known as the Hadamard product:

\[\vec{a} \odot \vec{b} = a_{ij}\, b_{ij}.\]

A second, dimension-reducing form of multiplication condenses two vectors into a single number — the inner product (or dot product): \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \sum a_i b_i\). A special case is the scalar–vector product: \(c\vec{a} = (ca_1, ca_2, \ldots, ca_n)\). In R, the inner product uses %*%.

The complementary operation, the outer product (or tensor product), expands two vectors into a matrix:

\[ \vec{a} \otimes \vec{b} = \mathbf{a}\mathbf{b}^{\!\top}. \]

In R, the outer product uses %o% or the outer() function. Beyond pure arithmetic, the outer product can produce all pairwise combinations of two vectors — a feature we shall exploit shortly.

a <- c(11, 12, 13, 14, 15)
b <- a + 4

# Element-wise (Hadamard) product
a * b
## [1] 165 192 221 252 285
# Inner (dot) product — returns a 1 × 1 matrix
a %*% b
##      [,1]
## [1,] 1115
# Outer (tensor) product — returns a matrix
a %o% b
##      [,1] [,2] [,3] [,4] [,5]
## [1,]  165  176  187  198  209
## [2,]  180  192  204  216  228
## [3,]  195  208  221  234  247
## [4,]  210  224  238  252  266
## [5,]  225  240  255  270  285

Vector “non-standard” operations

Mathematically, neither dot nor outer product has a unique inverse, so there is no division of vectors in the strict sense. In R, however, a / b is well-defined: it performs element-wise division, computing a[1]/b[1], a[2]/b[2], and so forth.

a <- 11:15
b <- a + 4

# Element-wise division
a / b
## [1] 0.7333333 0.7500000 0.7647059 0.7777778 0.7894737

When two vectors have unequal length, R automatically applies the recycling rule: elements of the shorter vector are reused in cycles to match the longer one. For example, with e <- c(1, 2) and a of length 5, R extends e to (1, 2, 1, 2, 1) before performing the operation. The same applies to addition, subtraction, and division.

a <- 11:15
e <- c(1, 2)

a * e
## [1] 11 24 13 28 15

R will also issue a warning, in effect saying: “you didn’t give me enough — I had to recycle.”

In addition, c() can construct character vectors; simply wrap each element in double quotes:

# Each element is wrapped in double quotes
aSentence <- c("I", "am", "from", "People's Republic of China", ".")
aSentence
## [1] "I"                          "am"                        
## [3] "from"                       "People's Republic of China"
## [5] "."

Exercise 2. The Sexagenary Cycle (Heavenly Stems and Earthly Branches).

China’s traditional calendar uses a “Heavenly Stem + Earthly Branch” system, formed by combining the ten Heavenly Stems with the twelve Earthly Branches. There are \(10 \times 12 = 120\) possible combinations in principle, but only sixty form the canonical cycle: by ancestral convention, Yang stems pair only with Yang branches, and Yin stems with Yin branches (where odd-positioned entries are Yang and even-positioned ones are Yin).

Use the operations introduced above to derive the sixty pairs in R.

  • Heavenly Stems: 甲, 乙, 丙, 丁, 戊, 己, 庚, 辛, 壬, 癸
  • Earthly Branches: 子, 丑, 寅, 卯, 辰, 巳, 午, 未, 申, 酉, 戌, 亥
tiangan <- c("甲", "乙", "丙", "丁", "戊", "己", "庚", "辛", "壬", "癸")
dizhi   <- c("子", "丑", "寅", "卯", "辰", "巳", "午", "未", "申", "酉", "戌", "亥")

Hints

  • Use seq() together with the modulo operator %% to separate Yang from Yin.
  • outer() generates all pairwise combinations of two vectors.
  • To output the actual stem-branch text rather than positional indices, use paste0(), which concatenates two strings — for example, paste0("甲", "子") returns "甲子".
tiangan <- c("甲", "乙", "丙", "丁", "戊", "己", "庚", "辛", "壬", "癸")
dizhi   <- c("子", "丑", "寅", "卯", "辰", "巳", "午", "未", "申", "酉", "戌", "亥")

# Step 1: split Yang (odd-indexed) and Yin (even-indexed) entries
tg_yang <- tiangan[seq(tiangan) %% 2 == 1]
tg_yin  <- tiangan[seq(tiangan) %% 2 == 0]

dz_yang <- dizhi[seq(dizhi) %% 2 == 1]
dz_yin  <- dizhi[seq(dizhi) %% 2 == 0]

# Step 2: outer product with paste0 to build all valid pairings
outer(tg_yang, dz_yang, FUN = paste0)
outer(tg_yin,  dz_yin,  FUN = paste0)

Matrices

Vectors are one-dimensional — a single sequence of numbers. Matrices are the two-dimensional structures built from multiple vectors. In a math textbook, a matrix takes the form

\[ A_{2 \times 2} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}. \]

In R the same matrix appears as:

# Create a 2-row matrix; data fill column by column
matrix(c(1, 2, 3, 4), nrow = 2)
##      [,1] [,2]
## [1,]    1    3
## [2,]    2    4

The two are nearly indistinguishable — R even labels the row and column indices on your behalf.

The command matrix(c(1, 2, 3, 4), nrow = 2) reads in plain English: “make a matrix from the data c(1, 2, 3, 4) arranged in 2 rows.” nrow is shorthand for “number of rows”, a common R abbreviation. One may equivalently specify ncol (number of columns); only one of the two is required, and R computes the other automatically.

R fills matrices in column-major order — the first column first, then the second, and so on — consistent with FORTRAN but opposite to the row-first convention common in mathematical exposition. The recycling rule also applies during construction: if too few elements are supplied, R loops over the input until the matrix is full, accompanied by a warning.

# Asking for a 2-row matrix while supplying only three elements
matrix(1:3, nrow = 2)
##      [,1] [,2]
## [1,]    1    3
## [2,]    2    1

Matrix elements are referenced by row and column subscripts. In mathematics, \(A_{12}\) denotes row 1, column 2. The R equivalent is A[1, 2]. Leaving a position blank means “all”: A[1, ] selects the entire first row, and A[, 2] selects the entire second column.

A <- matrix(1:3, nrow = 2)

# A specific element: row 1, column 2
A[1, 2]
## [1] 3
# An entire row
A[1, ]
## [1] 1 3
# An entire column
A[, 2]
## [1] 3 1

Single-Matrix Operations

The most common operation on a single matrix is transposition — interchanging its rows and columns. The transpose of \(\mathbf{A}\) is conventionally denoted \(\mathbf{A}^{\!\top}\). In R, transposition is performed by t(), named after the first letter of transposition.

A <- matrix(1:3, nrow = 2)

# View the original matrix
A
##      [,1] [,2]
## [1,]    1    3
## [2,]    2    1
# A single transpose
t(A)
##      [,1] [,2]
## [1,]    1    2
## [2,]    3    1
# Two transpositions return the original
t(t(A))
##      [,1] [,2]
## [1,]    1    3
## [2,]    2    1

Nested function calls

The expression t(t(A)) illustrates a common R idiom: nested function calls. The basic principle is evaluate from the inside out. R first runs t(A), then feeds that result into the outer t(), repeating outwards until no further wrapping remains. The logic is identical to mathematical function composition such as \(g(f(x))\).

Matrix Operations

Matrix operations parallel their vector analogues:

  • Addition and subtraction require matrices of the same dimensions: \(A \pm B = a_{ij} \pm b_{ij}\).
  • Multiplication comes in several flavours. The element-wise (Hadamard) product, inner product, and outer product all mirror their vector counterparts. For two matrices, the inner product (%*%) requires that the columns of the first matrix equal the rows of the second.
  • R further provides crossprod(), which computes the cross-product \(X^{\!\top} X\) or \(X^{\!\top} Y\) — used for Gram and covariance matrices, and not identical to the mathematical outer product.

The example below uses three matrices:

  • B: a 2-row matrix containing 1 to 6;
  • C: a 2-row matrix containing 6 to 1;
  • D: a 3-row matrix containing 6 to 1.

Addition and subtraction can be performed only between B and C (matching dimensions). Element-wise and outer products work analogously. The inner product requires cols-of-first = rows-of-second, so it can be performed only between B and D.

B <- matrix(1:6, nrow = 2)
C <- matrix(6:1, nrow = 2)
D <- matrix(6:1, nrow = 3)

## [2,]    5    2

# Basic operations (matching dimensions required)

##      [,1] [,2] [,3]

Solving Linear Systems

Why bother with matrix operations? Because data frames — the workhorse data structure of later chapters — are conceptually a special kind of matrix. Many operations defined for matrices (such as t()) extend to data frames, and many statistical methods reduce to linear-algebraic operations. Solving a multivariate equation, for instance, is mechanically very similar to estimating multivariate regression coefficients.

Consider the three-equation system

\[ \begin{cases} a + b + c = 6, \\ 3a + 2b + 4c = 9, \\ 2a + 2b - 6c = 4. \end{cases} \]

Letting matrix B hold the coefficients, \(\vec{y}\) the right-hand side, and \(\vec{x}\) the unknowns, the system becomes

\[ \vec{y} = \mathbf{B} \vec{x}, \qquad \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 9 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 3 & 2 & 4 \\ 2 & 2 & -6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{bmatrix}. \]

This bears a striking resemblance to a regression equation:

\[ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \cdots + \beta_n x_n + \epsilon. \]

In R, solve() (English: solve) yields the solution in a single line:

# Coefficient matrix B (3 × 3)
# Each column corresponds to the parameters of a, b, c
B <- matrix(c(1, 3, 2, 1, 2, 2, 1, 4, -6), nrow = 3)
B
##      [,1] [,2] [,3]
## [1,]    1    1    1
## [2,]    3    2    4
## [3,]    2    2   -6
# Constant vector y — the right-hand side: 6, 9, 4
y <- c(6, 9, 4)

# Solve the system: find x such that B %*% x = y
x <- solve(B, y)
x
## [1] -5 10  1

Solving a system of equations is a direct stepping stone to fitting a regression model — a connection we shall return to in later chapters.

Summary

This chapter introduces a number of concepts. Some need only an initial impression; their full meaning will deepen as later chapters revisit them in concrete settings. Others form the bedrock of what follows — failing to grasp them now will hinder later understanding.

The mathematical density of this chapter may feel heavy, but do not be intimidated. The aim is not to turn you into a mathematician; it is to help you understand the underlying logic by which R represents and processes data. R was designed for statistical computing, and statistical computing leans heavily on linear algebra. Vectors and matrices therefore become R’s basic data containers, and Boolean, vector, and matrix operations together form the substrate of regression, clustering, and many other methods we shall meet later.

Four core modules to remember:

  • Assignment: <-
  • Boolean logic
    • Comparisons: == (equal), != (not equal), >, <
    • Connectives: & (and), | (or), ! (not)
  • Vectors and indexing
    • Construction: c()
    • Sequence: seq()
    • Indexing: []
  • Matrices
    • Construction: matrix()
    • Dimensions: nrow(), ncol()
    • Transposition: t()
    • Solving / inversion: solve()
